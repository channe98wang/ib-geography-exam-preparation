Additionally, its benefits are not felt uniformly by the entirety of a city, and tensions can arise between those who were living in the are pre-regeneration/gentrification and the groups who were attracted here following the neighborhood's reinvestment.

Such an example can be seen in Detroit. Detroit used to have a population of around 1.8 million, and was the 4th largest city in the early to mid 20th century, Its role as the central hub for the automobile industry attracted thousands of people who were searching for a relatively lucrative job that did not require a degree. It remained a hub for decades, but rising tension between white and black people, and the increase amount of suburbanisation led to a decline in the population of Detroit and also an increase in the amount of abandoned houses a crime.

The government has been recently investing in the downtown and Midtown areas of Detroit, and has transformed its declining automobile industry, where the high rise office buildings in the downtown area has offered a place for automobile engineering. This has attracted many workers back into the city, and has transformed these areas into bustling areas with a large amount of foot traffic and the potential for businesses and economic growth.

However, many areas of Detroit remain poor and full of crime. The downtown and midtown demographic is predominantly white, and it came to be in this way following Detroit's reinvestment. Many of the original black residents express this discomfort, especially given the history of the two races in the city, and this has resulted in tensions between the two groups. Additionally, gentrification drives out the original residents of an area. Such a thing can be observed in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, where the residents of favelas were forced to move so that the land they lived one could be transformed into areas for the 2016 Rio Olympics.

Overall, this demonstrates that gentrification and regeneration can solve problems caused by urban decline by introducing better services and more opportunity into an area, but it also creates tensions between the old residents and the newcomers, it displaces those who used to live there, and it drives up prices that make it difficult for those who used live in the area.
