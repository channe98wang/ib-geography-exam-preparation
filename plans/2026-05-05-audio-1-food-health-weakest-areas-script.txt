Audio 1 script: weakest Food and health areas

Today is a Food and health repair session. From May 5 onward, the preparation plan says to stop expanding notes. That matters. The purpose of this audio is not to add new case studies or make a new Food and health textbook. The purpose is to tighten the answer habits most likely to lose marks: source precision, changing health-problem language, stakeholder chains, and evaluation of strategies.

Start with the short-answer issue. Food and health sources often show contrasts between food insecurity, child undernutrition, adult obesity, safe-water access, processed-food consumption, and disease burden. A weak answer says, "Region X is worse than Region Z." A stronger answer names the indicator and the data. For example: Region X has much higher food insecurity, at 38 percent, than Region Z, at 7 percent. Or: child undernutrition falls from 28 percent in Region X to 3 percent in Region Z, while adult obesity rises from 6 percent to 32 percent. That second sentence is especially useful because it shows a shift from undernutrition toward diet-related non-communicable disease risk.

Use the same rule as yesterday's Urban repair: category, indicator, number, comparison. If the question says "state two contrasts," write two compact data contrasts. If the question says "describe changing health problems," do not jump straight into causes. Describe the pattern first. You can write: the source suggests a transition from problems of limited access, weak storage, unsafe water, and undernutrition toward problems of processed-food consumption, obesity, and non-communicable disease. Then add evidence. Description earns marks when it is source-led.

Now tighten the content language. Food security has four dimensions: availability, access, utilization, and stability. Availability asks whether enough food exists. Access asks whether households can physically and economically obtain it. Utilization asks whether food is safe, nutritious, and usable by the body, which links to clean water, sanitation, disease, and diet quality. Stability asks whether access continues over time despite drought, conflict, price shocks, illness, job loss, or supply-chain disruption. Many answers lose marks because they treat food insecurity as only "not enough food." In IB Geography, food can be available nationally while some households remain insecure because prices, distribution, income, health, or water access are unequal.

The strongest Food and health answers often use the double burden of malnutrition. This means undernutrition and overnutrition can exist within the same country, region, city, or even household. One group may face hunger, stunting, micronutrient deficiency, unsafe water, or infectious disease risk. Another group may face obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, or other diet-related non-communicable diseases. A middle-income region can show both at once: child undernutrition falls, but adult obesity rises as processed foods become more available, urban lifestyles change, and fresh nutritious food may remain expensive or inaccessible for poorer households.

Be careful with "nutrition transition." It does not mean every place becomes healthier. It means diets and activity patterns change as incomes, urbanization, retail systems, advertising, transport, and food processing change. This can reduce some forms of hunger, but it can also increase consumption of high-sugar, high-salt, high-fat, ultra-processed foods. Use that idea when evaluating TNCs or supermarkets. Greater distribution can improve availability and convenience, but public-health outcomes depend on product quality, pricing, marketing, regulation, and consumer choice.

The next weak area is stakeholders. A stakeholder is not an answer by itself. A stakeholder must become a causal chain. Government -> regulation, school meals, sanitation, safety nets, healthcare, or farmer support -> better access or health outcome -> limitation such as cost, targeting, enforcement, corruption, or political priority. TNC -> investment, logistics, retail, jobs, processing, advertising -> improved distribution or lower prices -> limitation such as market power, processed-food marketing, farmer dependency, or health impacts. NGO -> emergency food, health education, vaccination, clean-water projects, or clinics -> quick support for vulnerable groups -> limitation such as donor dependence, short timescale, or failure to solve structural poverty.

Use the chain "actor -> action -> food-security dimension -> health outcome -> limitation." For example: governments can improve utilization by investing in safe water and sanitation, which reduces diarrhoeal disease and allows bodies to absorb nutrients more effectively, but the strategy is limited if infrastructure maintenance and rural access remain weak. That is a full answer because it names the stakeholder, the action, the mechanism, and the limitation. It is much stronger than "governments help people."

If a 10-mark question asks about TNCs, keep the evaluation balanced. TNCs can improve food security through investment, processing, storage, transport, retail distribution, jobs, and sometimes lower prices. They can also worsen health if marketing and pricing encourage ultra-processed diets, sugary drinks, or dependence on branded products. If you use Brazil and Nestle from the existing materials, do not invent statistics. Use it as a stakeholder-power example: distribution and access benefits must be weighed against processed-food marketing, nutrition transition, and public-health concerns. The judgement is not that TNCs are always good or always harmful. The judgement is that their impact depends on regulation, product quality, affordability, consumer information, and who controls the food system.

If a 10-mark question asks about strategies to improve food security or health, choose a strategy and evaluate success. Water and sanitation can improve health by reducing waterborne disease and improving nutrient absorption. School meals can improve access and child nutrition while supporting attendance. Disease prevention can reduce health inequality if vaccination, education, clinics, and clean water reach vulnerable groups. Small-farmer support can improve availability and livelihoods through credit, storage, seeds, irrigation, training, and market access. Food-waste reduction can improve efficiency, but it does not automatically help poor households unless recovered food, pricing, distribution, and access are managed.

Now practise the difference between description and explanation. Description: Region X has 38 percent food insecurity and 28 percent child undernutrition, while Region Z has 7 percent food insecurity and 3 percent child undernutrition. Explanation: Region X may have weaker safe-water access, lower incomes, poorer storage, or less reliable distribution, so households face both limited food access and higher disease risk. Evaluation: improving food availability alone may not solve the problem if water, sanitation, affordability, and healthcare remain weak. That three-step movement is what Food and health answers need.

Use "for whom" and "where" in every evaluation sentence. A strategy may work for urban consumers but not remote rural households. It may improve calorie access but not diet quality. It may reduce infectious disease but not obesity. It may help children through school meals but miss adults, migrants, informal workers, or people outside formal services. It may work in the short term during a crisis but fail if long-term funding disappears. Evaluation is not decoration at the end. It is a judgement about scale, group, time, and limitation.

Here is a compact model paragraph. Food and health strategies are most effective when they address access, utilization, and stability together rather than only increasing food supply. For example, water and sanitation improvements can reduce disease, improve nutrient absorption, and support safer food preparation, so they can improve both health and food security. However, the impact is limited if poorer or rural households still cannot afford varied food, reach clinics, or store food safely. Therefore, a strategy is strongest when infrastructure, affordability, health education, and targeted support work together; it is weaker when it solves only one part of the food-security chain.

Now apply that paragraph logic to a TNC question. A weak paragraph says that a TNC sells food and therefore improves food security. A stronger paragraph asks what part of food security changes. TNCs may improve availability because they increase processing, storage, logistics, and retail networks. They may improve access for some consumers if products become cheaper or more widely distributed. However, they do not automatically improve utilization, because diet quality depends on nutrition, water, sanitation, health knowledge, and product composition. If the most available products are sugary drinks, salty snacks, or highly processed foods, the result may be a shift from hunger toward obesity, diabetes, or cardiovascular disease risk. The evaluation sentence is: TNCs may improve market access, but they are only partly successful as health actors unless regulation, product quality, and consumer protection are strong.

Apply the same logic to government policy. A weak answer says government action is important. A stronger answer specifies the policy and the mechanism. A school meal programme can improve access for children, reduce short-term hunger, and support attendance. It may also improve utilization if meals are nutritious and linked to safe water. But it may miss children outside school, depend on public funding, or be disrupted by conflict, corruption, or poor supply chains. A public-health campaign can improve knowledge, but knowledge alone is weak if healthy food is unaffordable. A subsidy can reduce prices, but it may be poorly targeted or may support less healthy foods if the policy is not designed carefully. Always ask: what does the policy change, and what does it fail to change?

Disease is another Food and health area where students can become too general. The exam does not need a medical essay. It needs geographic mechanisms. Disease spreads or becomes more severe where water and sanitation are weak, housing is overcrowded, healthcare access is limited, nutrition is poor, vaccination is low, or mobility connects places. Food insecurity can worsen disease outcomes because malnutrition weakens the body, and disease can worsen food insecurity because illness reduces income, work, school attendance, and nutrient absorption. That two-way link is valuable. Food affects health, and health affects food security.

When using examples, keep them lean. You can use Brazil and Nestle if that is the class anchor, but do not force it into every question. For a strategy question, water and sanitation, school meals, disease prevention, or small-farmer support may be cleaner. For a stakeholder question, government, TNCs, NGOs, farmers, retailers, and consumers can be compared. For a health-inequality question, focus on income, gender, age, urban-rural access, safe water, healthcare, and processed-food environments. The best answer is not the one with the most examples. It is the one where every example answers the command term.

Build one final Food and health thesis. "Food and health strategies can improve outcomes when they target access, utilization, and stability together, but their success is uneven because income, water, sanitation, healthcare, regulation, and stakeholder power shape who benefits." That thesis can fit many questions. It does not lock you into one case. It gives you criteria, and criteria make evaluation easier.

Before leaving Food and health, check command terms. "Describe" means use the source pattern. "Explain" means give the mechanism. "Examine" means look at more than one factor or stakeholder. "Evaluate" means make a judgement about success and limitation. "To what extent" means decide the degree. If the command term is evaluate and the whole answer is descriptive, the ceiling is lower even if the content is accurate. Add judgement words early: partly, mainly, unevenly, more successful for, less effective where, limited by. These words force the answer to weigh evidence.

Here is a final command-term paragraph you can reuse. "A food-security strategy can improve health where it increases access to safe, nutritious food and reduces disease risk, but it is only partly successful if poorer households still face high prices, unsafe water, weak healthcare, or limited transport." This sentence works because it names the outcome, gives the condition for success, and gives the limitation. In the exam, adapt it to the source or case in front of you. If the question is about stakeholders, replace "strategy" with "stakeholder action." If it is about health inequality, replace "food-security strategy" with "health intervention." The logic stays the same: success depends on access, utilization, stability, and who is still excluded.

For short visual or data questions, use four moves. First, identify the highest and lowest values. Second, compare two regions with numbers. Third, name the health or food-security pattern. Fourth, avoid unsupported causes until the command term asks for them. For explain questions, then add the mechanism. For evaluate questions, add benefit plus limitation.

End with a memory drill. Say the four dimensions of food security: availability, access, utilization, stability. Say the double burden: undernutrition and obesity can coexist. Say the stakeholder chain: actor, action, food-security dimension, health outcome, limitation. Say one evaluative sentence: a strategy may improve access, but it is only partly successful if affordability, water, sanitation, healthcare, or regulation remain weak. That is the Food and health repair for today.

During the 25-minute block, Food and health is not the written task unless the visual source includes food or health impacts. The written task is a fast visual-stimulus response set. Bring this Food and health repair into that task by reading indicators carefully, comparing with data, and writing one clear policy limitation instead of a general claim.
