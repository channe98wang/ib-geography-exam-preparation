12. b) The rate at which agricultural innovation diffuse is highly dependent on geographic factors in the sense that an area adopting these new innovations is dependent on how well their geographic setting allows them to adopt these innovations. If an innovation is less effective in a specific area due to the area's geographic contexts, that area is less likely to adopt that innovation, and vice versa.

This can be seen through the green revolution. American scientist Norman Borlaug developed a seed in mexico that produced a higher yield of rice per seed than what was seen before. This seed, called a "high yield variety" was incredibly effective, and the production of grain and rice increased tremendously with this new invention. At this time, India had suffered from two sucesive droughts that decreased agricultural productivity tremendously. That, coupled by the rapidly increasing population in India, resulted in acute food security issues. However, an Indian scientist heard of what was happening in Mexico, and discovered that High yield varieties were incredibly suitable for Indian soil. This adoption of these seeds significantly increased the production of rice in India, and this seed was adopted nationally across India, which transformed India from a country that had to import their rice, to a self-sufficient producer on rice. Nowadays, India is one of the top exporters of rice worldwide, This goes to show how a country's climate and geography impacts the diffusion of agricultural innovation, as India only resorted to using HYVs due to their effectiveness and growing quality in these specific conditions.

Geographic factors also impact the rate of diffusion through acting as physical barriers. Structures like mountains, rivers, and oceans block the diffusion of ideas. This was especially prominent earlier on, where the world was significantly less connected and less technologically advanced. Ideas at this time were usually spread via human to human interactions. These geographic features physically stopped ideas from one side reaching the other side, and this is evidenced by the fact that different areas came up with similar innovations in a way that was completely seperate from each other.

Therefore, geographic factors act as physical barriers that stop the diffusion of innovation. They also determine the effectiveness of the innovation in a specific area, and thus, the rate and likelihood that people are going to adopt it, which directly influences the rate of diffusion

14. b) Centrifugal population movements have a great impact on the economic and social livelihood of a city's residential area. the movement of population away from these areas have a profound impact on the infrastructure of the area and economy, which in turn impacts the social life of those still living in these areas.

This can be seen in Detroit, where the decentralization of its automobile industry, the tensions between the wite and black citizens, and the popularization of "the american dream" resulted in large scale suburbanization, where the white people moved to the outskirts of Detroit, leaving the residential areas in its city proper significantly more empty than before. What used to be a city of around 1.7 million at its peak turned to a place with a significantly lower population, and this lower population can still be observed today, with around 670,000 living in the city of Detroit today. This large scale suburbanization made it so that there was not enough people in the actual city of Detroit to sustain its economy, especially since many key assembly plants for cars moved alongside the people to the suburbs, which significantly impacted the city's economy as these plants were no longer located in the actual Detroit. Additionally, the lack of people made it much more difficult to upkeep infrastructure, as less people resulted in less tax revenue, and the cost of maintenance became increasingly more significant. This declining economy and poor infrastructure upkeep resulted in increased poverty rates, which significantly increased crime rates. This directly made the residential areas less safe and increased social tensions,

However, there does exist benefits for centrifugal movement. For example, the movement of more affluent citizens to an area reduce competition to the less wealthy that stayed in the area. This reduction in competition generally leads to a reduction in prices, making the quality of life for the citizens staying in the area significantly better. Additionally, a less population generally relaxes the burden on resources and infrastructure, allowing for a larger amount of resources being available per person.

In general centrifugal movements places a burden on residential areas by decreasing economic productivity, which makes infrastructure harder to upkeep and leads to poverty and increased social tensions through increased crime rates.
