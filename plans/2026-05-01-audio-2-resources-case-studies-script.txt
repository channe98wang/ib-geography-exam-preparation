Friday 2026-05-01. Audio 2. Global resource consumption and security: case
studies and stewardship examples.

Minute 0 to 2: resource case-study discipline.

This audio is for the Paper 2 core unit Global resource consumption and
security. The plan asks for Resources case studies and stewardship examples.
Use the same case-study chain as Urban environments: place, issue, causes,
impacts, response, evaluation. For Resources, add two extra questions: secure
for whom, and sustainable for how long?

Resource security means reliable, affordable, safe, and acceptable access over
time. Stewardship means responsible management of resources for present and
future users. Stewardship can include conservation, recycling, repair,
reuse, efficient technology, demand management, pollution control, regulation,
pricing, producer responsibility, and public trust. It is not just "using less."
It is managing a resource system so that costs and benefits are not dumped onto
other people, other places, or the future.

The student case-study sheet lists one resource anchor: China no longer
accepting our e-waste. That should be protected because it links high
consumption, waste trade, pollution risk, circular economy, and uneven
responsibility. A second strong stewardship anchor is Singapore water security.
China's waste-import restrictions show what happens when a linear economy is
disrupted. Singapore's water system shows how long-term planning can improve
resource security despite physical scarcity.

Today, keep three case cells ready. First, the global e-waste scale: 62 billion
kilograms of e-waste were generated globally in 2022, with 22.3 percent
formally collected and recycled, according to the Global E-waste Monitor 2024.
Second, China changed waste import policy in 2018, banning 24 types of solid
waste in four classes, including unsorted wastepaper and scrap plastic. Third,
Singapore uses the Four National Taps: local catchment, imported water, NEWater,
and desalinated water.

Memory check. Define stewardship in one sentence. Then say: secure for whom,
at what scale, and for how long?

Minute 2 to 5: China, e-waste, and the linear economy.

The China waste case starts with consumption outside China as well as policy
inside China. High-consuming societies buy electronics, packaging, paper, and
manufactured goods. When products become waste, some materials can be reused
or recycled, but only if collection, sorting, processing, labor protection, and
pollution control are strong. For years, many exporting countries relied on
China and other destinations to absorb recyclable or waste material. This
shifted part of the environmental burden away from the places where consumption
occurred.

In early 2018, China's import ban on selected solid wastes came into effect.
The State Council report says China banned 24 types of solid waste in four
classes, including unsorted wastepaper and scrap plastic, due to environmental
concerns. The same report says stricter permit controls followed and that many
exporting countries had to find new destinations or handle more material
themselves. Later policy changes tightened the system further. For exam use,
the key point is not just the number 24. The key point is that one country's
environmental regulation exposed the dependence of other countries on exporting
waste instead of designing a circular system at home.

Now connect it to global e-waste. The Global E-waste Monitor 2024 reports that
the world generated 62 billion kilograms of e-waste in 2022, equal to 7.8
kilograms per person on average. Only 22.3 percent was documented as formally
collected and recycled in an environmentally sound way. It also says global
e-waste rose from 34 billion kilograms in 2010 to 62 billion kilograms in
2022, while formal collection and recycling rose much more slowly. This is a
strong resource-security point because e-waste contains valuable materials as
well as hazardous substances. Poor management wastes metals and creates health
and pollution risks.

Use the China case in a six-link chain. Place: China and waste-exporting
countries such as the United States, Japan, Germany, or Britain, depending on
the source. Issue: imported recyclable and waste materials, including e-waste
and related solid waste, created pollution and management pressure. Causes:
high consumption, short product life cycles, weak repair culture, cheap
shipping, unequal recycling capacity, and demand for raw materials. Impacts:
pollution risk in receiving areas, disruption for exporters after the ban,
stockpiling, landfill or incineration pressure, and the need for domestic
recycling reform. Response: import bans, stricter permits, circular economy,
extended producer responsibility, design for repair, collection systems, and
domestic processing. Evaluation: the ban reduced one pathway of pollution for
China, but it did not solve global waste generation; responsibility shifted
back to producers, consumers, and exporting countries.

That final evaluation is essential. Do not write that China's policy solved
e-waste. It changed incentives and geography. A strong answer says the case
shows how a linear economy creates hidden dependencies. Stewardship requires
reducing waste at source, designing longer-life products, building safe
recycling systems, and making producers or consumers responsible for end-of-life
costs.

Minute 5 to 8: Singapore water stewardship.

Singapore is a strong resource stewardship case because it lacks large natural
freshwater sources but has used planning, technology, infrastructure, pricing,
and public management to improve water security. The Singapore Ministry of
Sustainability and the Environment says Singapore is among the most
water-stressed countries in the world because it lacks large rivers, natural
springs, glaciers, and land for collecting water. The response is the Four
National Taps: local catchment, imported water, NEWater, and desalinated water.

Store the useful statistics. About two-thirds of Singapore's land surface
serves as water catchment. Rainwater is moved through more than 8,000
kilometers of drains and canals to 17 reservoirs. Imported water from Johor is
allowed under the 1962 Water Agreement, with up to 250 million gallons of raw
water per day, and that agreement expires in 2061. NEWater was introduced in
2002 and is produced using microfiltration, reverse osmosis, and disinfection.
It has passed more than 150,000 scientific tests and Singapore currently has
five NEWater factories. Desalination is another tap, and Singapore has five
desalination plants.

Now add demand and limitation. Singapore consumes about 440 million gallons of
water per day, and total demand is expected to almost double by 2065 as
population and the economy grow. Household water consumption fell from 165
liters per capita per day in 2003 to 141 in 2019, rose during the COVID period,
and returned to 141 in 2023. Desalinated water is weather-resilient, but it is
energy intensive. Imported water improves supply, but it creates dependence on
an external agreement. NEWater improves resilience, but it needs technology,
energy, monitoring, public trust, and industrial compliance.

Build the six-link chain. Place: Singapore. Issue: water security in a small,
land-scarce, highly urbanized country. Causes: limited natural freshwater,
limited land for catchment, high demand, economic growth, population needs, and
climate uncertainty. Impacts: risk of dependence, drought vulnerability,
competition between domestic and industrial water use, and the need for reliable
infrastructure. Response: Four National Taps, demand management, water pricing,
efficiency labels, water recycling, NEWater, desalination, catchment expansion,
drainage, and public education. Evaluation: Singapore shows that scarcity can
be managed through governance and technology, but the strategy is costly and
energy intensive, and long-term security still depends on demand reduction,
public trust, and climate resilience.

This case is excellent for the Malthusian and Boserupian debate. A Malthusian
view would emphasize physical limits: small land area, no large rivers, rising
demand, and climate uncertainty. A Boserupian view would emphasize innovation:
NEWater, desalination, catchment expansion, efficiency, and governance. The
best answer weighs both. Technology can reduce scarcity, but it does not remove
trade-offs in energy use, cost, dependency, and unequal capacity between places.

Minute 8 to 10: comparing the two resource cases.

Compare China e-waste and Singapore water because the contrast is strong. The
China case starts with overconsumption and waste disposal in a globalized
economy. The Singapore case starts with physical scarcity and the need for
planned supply. China is useful for circular economy, externalized costs, trade,
pollution, and product life cycles. Singapore is useful for water security,
stewardship, technology, demand management, and governance.

The two cases also show two different meanings of resource security. In the
e-waste case, security is about recovering materials safely and avoiding
pollution. Valuable metals are present, but poor systems mean they are lost or
handled dangerously. In the Singapore case, security is about reliable water
supply despite limited local resources. The resource is physically limited, but
planning can improve access.

Use the same evaluation criteria for both: effectiveness, equity, cost, time
scale, and side effects. China's import restrictions may be effective for
reducing foreign waste inflows and local pollution risk, but side effects
include displaced waste flows and pressure on exporting countries that lack
domestic recycling capacity. Singapore's water strategy is effective for
diversifying supply, but it is expensive, technology dependent, and some taps
are energy intensive. Equity also matters. A wealthy city-state may afford
advanced desalination and reclaimed water systems; many water-stressed places
cannot copy the model without finance, technical capacity, governance, and
public trust.

This comparison gives you a strong Paper 2 paragraph. Resource stewardship is
most successful when it changes the whole system rather than moving pressure
elsewhere. China's waste-import restrictions changed the geography of waste,
but global e-waste keeps rising unless consumption, design, repair, and formal
recycling improve. Singapore's water strategy improves security through
diversified supply and demand management, but it must manage energy use and
future demand. Therefore, stewardship is not a single project; it is long-term
governance of production, consumption, waste, technology, and behavior.

Add the water-energy-food nexus whenever the source points toward linked
systems. Singapore's water case is not only about water. NEWater and
desalination need energy, industry needs reliable water, and climate
uncertainty can affect both supply and demand. E-waste is also a nexus issue
because electronics contain metals, plastics, energy embodied in manufacturing,
and pollution risks at disposal. A strong Paper 2 answer can say that resource
security is connected across systems: solving one pressure can create another
unless stewardship considers the full chain from extraction to consumption to
waste.

Now add theory. A Malthusian or neo-Malthusian answer emphasizes limits:
population growth, rising consumption, finite water, waste accumulation,
pollution, and environmental thresholds. A Boserupian or anti-Malthusian answer
emphasizes innovation: water recycling, desalination, circular economy,
efficiency, product redesign, and better governance. Do not memorize one side
as correct. Use cases to judge. Singapore supports the innovation argument, but
only because it has finance, technical capacity, institutions, and public
trust. Global e-waste supports the limits argument because technology use is
rising faster than formal recycling. The strongest answer says that innovation
can reduce resource insecurity, but it is uneven between places and may create
new costs.

For a short response, keep the chain tight. Example one: global e-waste reached
62 billion kilograms in 2022, but only 22.3 percent was formally collected and
recycled; therefore valuable materials are lost and hazardous substances create
pollution risk. China tightening waste imports exposed the weakness of relying
on exports, so a circular economy is needed. However, that requires regulation,
producer responsibility, repairable design, and safe recycling infrastructure.
Example two: Singapore's limited natural freshwater creates water-security
risk, but the Four National Taps diversify supply. However, desalination is
energy intensive and future demand is expected to almost double by 2065, so
conservation and efficiency remain essential.

Minute 10 to 12: exam-ready resource chains.

For a visual stimulus on resource consumption, use this order. First, describe
the visible trend or pattern. Second, name the resource pressure: water, energy,
food, minerals, waste, or land. Third, explain the driver: population growth,
income growth, urbanization, consumer culture, technology, diet, or trade.
Fourth, state the impact: insecurity, price, pollution, health risk, emissions,
ecosystem stress, or conflict risk. Fifth, add stewardship and evaluation.

If the source shows rising waste, use the e-waste chain: rising consumption and
short product life cycles increase e-waste; formal recycling has not kept up;
hazardous and valuable materials are mismanaged; China's waste restrictions
show that exporting waste is not a sustainable solution; circular economy and
producer responsibility can help, but only with collection systems, regulation,
repair, design change, and safe processing.

If the source shows water scarcity, use the Singapore chain: limited local
freshwater and rising demand create water-security risk; Singapore responds
with local catchment, imported water, NEWater, desalination, conservation, and
pricing; this improves resilience, but the strategy is expensive and partly
energy intensive, so demand management and continued innovation remain
important.

When you choose which case to use, match the case to the question. If the
question asks about unequal consumption, e-waste is stronger because it shows
how high-consuming societies can shift waste and pollution pressures elsewhere.
If the question asks about resource management in a place with physical
scarcity, Singapore water is stronger. If the question asks about stewardship
in general, compare both: China shows the limits of a linear system, while
Singapore shows the strengths and costs of planned circular or diversified
management. That comparison is useful because it prevents a one-example answer
from sounding universal.

Also protect the word "security." Do not use it as a synonym for "supply."
Security includes reliability, affordability, quality, acceptability, and
stability over time. In e-waste, security can mean recovering metals safely and
reducing pollution risk. In water, security means reliable clean water for
households, industry, and public services even during dry periods or political
uncertainty. This wider definition helps you explain why governance matters as
much as physical resources.

Keep command terms precise. Describe the pattern. Explain the cause. Analyse
the relationship between consumption, security, and impact. Evaluate the
response by asking how well it works, for whom, at what cost, and with what
side effects. That is the difference between a memorized case and an exam-ready
case.
