\subsection{Possible Plan After Timed Attempt}

Use this only after you have made your own plan.

\noindent\colorbox{SoftTint}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.965\textwidth}
\sffamily
\textbf{\textcolor{HeadingBlue}{Prompt A possible judgment.}}
Urban management strategies can reduce selected social and environmental
stresses, but their success is partial unless they address inequality as well
as the physical environment.
\end{minipage}
}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Paragraph 1: Environmental strategies such as public transport, green
  infrastructure, waste systems, or brownfield redevelopment can reduce
  pollution, congestion, flood risk, or dereliction.
  \item Paragraph 2: Social strategies such as affordable housing, service
  upgrading, and tenant protection are needed because environmental
  improvements alone can still leave displacement or inequality.
  \item Paragraph 3: Evaluation should compare stakeholders and scale. Success
  may be stronger for commuters, investors, or central districts than for
  renters, informal workers, or peripheral households.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\colorbox{SoftBlue}{%
\begin{minipage}{0.965\textwidth}
\sffamily
\textbf{\textcolor{HeadingBlue}{Prompt B possible judgment.}}
Sustainable urban management can solve some future urban-growth challenges to a
large extent when it is integrated, inclusive, and long term, but it cannot
fully solve challenges created by poverty, land markets, rapid growth, and weak
governance.
\end{minipage}
}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Paragraph 1: Compact and transit-oriented planning can reduce sprawl,
  car dependence, and infrastructure costs.
  \item Paragraph 2: Green and resilient infrastructure can reduce heat, flood,
  pollution, and resource pressures.
  \item Paragraph 3: Limits include affordability, displacement, digital
  exclusion, maintenance costs, and the uneven power of stakeholders.
\end{itemize}
