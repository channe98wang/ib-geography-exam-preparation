\section{Strict Marking Rules}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Mark only what was written during the timed attempt.
  \item For \exam{state}, give one mark for each correct source-based point.
  \item For \exam{describe}, reward patterns, contrasts, and exact evidence.
  \item For \exam{explain}, require a cause-and-effect chain.
  \item For 10-mark answers, reward named examples, geographic processes,
  balance, and a clear judgment.
\end{itemize}

\section{Urban Environments Mark Guide}

\subsection{Question 1: State Evidence [2]}

Award up to \term{2 marks}, one for each valid piece of evidence. Possible
answers include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Frequent transit access is \term{91\%} in the central high-density core
  but only \term{18\%} in the informal hillside settlement.
  \item The informal hillside settlement has the highest air-pollution index,
  \term{83}, and only \term{2 m$^2$} green space per person.
  \item The outer car-dependent suburb has a much longer average commute,
  \term{67 minutes}, than the central core, at \term{34 minutes}.
  \item The transit corridor suburb has better transit access, \term{74\%},
  than the outer suburb, at \term{26\%}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 2: Describe Two Contrasts [4]}

Award up to \term{4 marks}: up to \term{2 marks} for each clear contrast using
both zones and source evidence.

\subsection{Question 3: Explain Transport Stress [4]}

Award up to \term{4 marks}: up to \term{2 marks} for each explained chain.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Car dependence can increase fuel use and traffic emissions, worsening
  air quality and contributing to urban environmental stress.
  \item Long commutes can reduce access to jobs, education, and services,
  especially for lower-income households without reliable transport.
  \item Transit investment can raise land values around stations, which may
  improve access but also increase rent pressure and displacement risk.
  \item Road expansion and low-density sprawl can consume land, reduce farmland
  or habitats, and increase infrastructure costs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 4(a): Sustainable Transport [10]}

Credit any valid named city. Curitiba is a strong example if the answer links
BRT, public transport access, congestion, cost, ridership, maintenance, and
equity. Other valid examples may include Singapore, London, Bogota, or a
class-specific case.

\subsection{Question 4(b): Sprawl And Density [10]}

Reward balanced judgment. Strong answers compare sprawl challenges such as car
dependence, land consumption, infrastructure cost, and segregation with
high-density challenges such as congestion, pollution, overcrowding, and high
land values.

\section{Food And Health Mark Guide}

\subsection{Question 1: State Two Contrasts [2]}

Award up to \term{2 marks}, one for each valid source-based contrast.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Food insecurity is highest in the dryland smallholder zone,
  \term{46\%}, and lowest in the irrigated core, \term{12\%}.
  \item Safe water access is highest in the irrigated core, \term{93\%}, and
  lowest in the dryland smallholder zone, \term{54\%}.
  \item Child wasting is \term{21\%} in the dryland smallholder zone but only
  \term{5\%} in the irrigated core.
  \item Food prices increased by \term{36\%} in the dryland smallholder zone
  compared with \term{8\%} in the irrigated core.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 2: Describe Patterns [4]}

Award up to \term{4 marks}: up to \term{2 marks} for each described pattern or
contrast.

\begin{itemize}
  \item The dryland smallholder zone is the most vulnerable overall, with the
  highest food insecurity, child wasting, food-price increase, and the lowest
  safe water access.
  \item The irrigated core has the lowest food insecurity and child wasting and
  the highest safe water access, but its main issue is high water use.
  \item The urban low-income district has moderate food insecurity,
  \term{31\%}, and a large food-price increase, \term{28\%}, suggesting an
  affordability problem.
  \item The export farming belt has better average indicators than the dryland
  zone, but Source B still suggests uneven access for landless workers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 3: Explain Climate-Shock Effects [4]}

Award up to \term{4 marks}: up to \term{2 marks} for each explained chain.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Drought can reduce crop yields and local food supply, raising prices
  and reducing household access to sufficient food.
  \item Water scarcity can reduce sanitation and safe drinking water, increasing
  diarrhoeal disease risk and worsening undernutrition.
  \item Food-price rises can force poorer households to buy cheaper,
  lower-quality foods, increasing malnutrition or diet-related health problems.
  \item Loss of farm income can reduce spending on healthcare, education, and
  food, deepening vulnerability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Question 4(a): Malnutrition And Disease [10]}

Credit named examples such as Haiti cholera, a class disease case, or another
valid place. Strong answers explain two-way links: malnutrition weakens
immunity, disease reduces nutrient absorption, and unsafe water or poverty can
connect both.

\subsection{Question 4(b): Food-Security Strategies [10]}

Credit valid strategies such as irrigation, drought-resistant crops, food
storage, cash transfers, school meals, WASH programmes, early warning, food
price support, or crop diversification. Reward evaluation of cost, equity,
scale, sustainability, and local suitability.

\midrule
