Audio 2 script: Population data, models, and comparison points

This audio keeps Paper 2 active while Paper 1 is being repaired. The focus is Population distribution - changing population, especially data, models, and common comparison points. The goal is not to memorize every demographic detail. The goal is to look at a table or graph and quickly say what the data show, what model or process it links to, and what consequences follow.

Start with the core vocabulary. Population distribution means where people are located. Population density means people per unit area, usually people per square kilometre. Population growth is the change in total population over time, shaped by birth rate, death rate, and net migration. Fertility rate means the average number of children born per woman. Replacement fertility is often treated as about 2.1 children per woman in many countries, but actual replacement level can vary with mortality and sex ratio. Natural increase is births minus deaths. Net migration is immigration minus emigration. Population momentum means population can keep growing even after fertility falls because a large young cohort is entering reproductive age.

When you see a population data table, read it in layers. First, identify the extremes. Which country has the highest fertility, the oldest median age, the fastest growth, or the largest elderly share? Second, identify contrasts. Is one country youthful while another is ageing? Is growth driven by fertility, by migration, or by both? Third, explain consequences. A youthful structure can create pressure on schools, healthcare, housing, sanitation, and future jobs, but it can also create a demographic dividend if education and employment are strong. An ageing structure can increase healthcare, pensions, dependency ratios, and labour shortages, but it can also reflect high life expectancy and better health systems.

Use the demographic transition model as a thinking tool. In early transition, birth and death rates are high, so growth is low or unstable. In the second stage, death rates fall while birth rates remain high, so population grows rapidly. In the third stage, birth rates fall due to urbanization, education, contraception, lower infant mortality, and changing social expectations. In the fourth stage, both birth and death rates are low, so population growth slows. Some countries are described as being in a fifth stage, with very low fertility, ageing, and possible population decline. The model is useful, but it is not a perfect prediction. Migration, conflict, policy, disease, inequality, and culture can change the pattern.

Now connect data to the model. A country with fertility of 4.6, median age of 18, and annual growth of 2.7 percent is likely youthful and in a relatively early or middle transition stage. A country with fertility of 1.3, median age of 48, and negative growth is ageing and may be in a late or post-transition stage. A country with fertility near 2.1 can still grow if it has positive net migration or population momentum. That last point is important because students often assume replacement fertility means no growth immediately. It does not. Age structure matters.

Population pyramids work the same way. A wide base usually means high birth rates and a youthful population. A narrow base with a larger middle or top suggests lower fertility and ageing. A bulge in working ages can suggest past high fertility, migration, or a demographic dividend opportunity. A sudden dent can suggest war, famine, disease, out-migration, or a past fall in births. You do not need to diagnose everything. You need to describe the shape, support it with age groups, and explain a likely consequence.

Comparison language is the exam skill. Avoid vague statements like "Country A is younger and Country B is older." Add data. Write: Country A has a median age of 18 and 43 percent under 15, whereas Country B has a median age of 48 and 29 percent aged 65 and over. That sentence is worth more because it identifies indicators and contrasts the two places. Then explain: this means Country A is likely to face greater pressure on schools and future job creation, while Country B faces greater pension, healthcare, and labour-supply pressure.

For migration, separate push and pull factors, but do not leave them as lists. A push factor is something encouraging people to leave, such as unemployment, insecurity, low wages, environmental stress, or weak services. A pull factor is something attracting people, such as jobs, education, safety, higher wages, family networks, or better healthcare. The explanation chain is: factor -> movement -> demographic or spatial outcome. For example, rural poverty and limited services can push young adults toward cities, increasing urban growth while leaving some rural areas with ageing or labour shortages.

For population policy, think in terms of aims, methods, and limits. Pronatalist policies try to raise fertility through childcare support, parental leave, housing help, tax incentives, or work-family reform. Antinatalist policies try to lower fertility through contraception access, education, later marriage, female empowerment, or sometimes coercive rules. Migration policy can increase or limit population growth, fill labour shortages, or manage border pressures. The evaluation is always about effectiveness and side effects. Does the policy change behaviour? Is it fair? Is it affordable? Does it respect rights? Does it solve the underlying cause?

The demographic dividend is another high-value idea. A demographic dividend can happen when fertility falls and the share of working-age people rises relative to dependents. It is not automatic. It needs education, healthcare, jobs, gender equality, infrastructure, political stability, and investment. Without those, a large young workforce can become unemployment pressure rather than a dividend. This is a useful evaluation point because it stops the answer from assuming that youthful populations are only a problem or only an opportunity.

Ageing also needs balanced evaluation. Ageing can increase dependency and costs, but older populations are not simply a burden. Older people may have savings, skills, political influence, and demand for services that create jobs. Countries can respond with immigration, later retirement, automation, healthcare reform, pension reform, family support, and age-friendly planning. A strong answer says ageing creates pressure, but outcomes depend on policy, productivity, health, and social systems.

Now practise with a model table in your head. Country Alpha has high fertility, low median age, high growth, and many under 15. Country Beta has low fertility, high median age, negative growth, and many aged 65 and over. Country Gamma has fertility near replacement, moderate median age, positive migration, and moderate growth. The strongest comparison is not "Alpha is developing and Beta is developed." That may be too broad and may rely on assumptions. The strongest comparison is source-based: Alpha is more youthful and growing faster, while Beta is older and shrinking slightly. Then use geography to explain possible reasons and consequences.

For command terms, keep the difference clear. State means give a short answer, usually one fact. Describe means say what the data show. Explain means give reasons or consequences with causal links. Suggest means give a plausible reason from the source and your knowledge. Compare means show similarities and differences. Evaluate means weigh success and limitations. In Paper 2 structured questions, many marks are lost when students explain before describing, or when they describe without using the data.

A useful formula for data description is "overall, highest, lowest, exception." Overall, population growth is higher where fertility and youth share are higher. The highest growth is in Alpha at 2.7 percent. The lowest growth is in Beta at negative 0.3 percent. An exception or nuance is Gamma, where fertility is close to replacement but growth continues, possibly because of net in-migration and population momentum. That four-part formula can turn a table into a clear answer quickly.

Now turn that formula into a model answer. Suppose the question says: using the source, describe two contrasts between Country Alpha and Country Beta. A weak answer says: Alpha has more young people and Beta has more old people. A stronger answer says: Country Alpha has a youthful population structure, with a median age of 18 and 43 percent of people aged under 15, whereas Country Beta is ageing, with a median age of 48 and 29 percent aged 65 and over. A second contrast is that Alpha is growing rapidly at 2.7 percent per year, while Beta is declining slightly at negative 0.3 percent; this is linked to Alpha's much higher fertility of 4.6 compared with Beta's 1.3. That answer uses the source, compares directly, and avoids unsupported causes.

Suppose the next question asks for consequences. Start with the age structure, then link to demand. For Alpha, a youthful population can increase demand for schools, vaccinations, maternal healthcare, housing, water, sanitation, and future employment because a large child cohort raises the youth dependency burden. It can become an opportunity only if education and job creation convert that cohort into a productive workforce. For Beta, ageing can increase demand for pensions, healthcare, social care, and age-friendly transport, while a smaller working-age population may create labour shortages. But the impact depends on productivity, retirement age, savings, immigration, and healthcare efficiency. That last sentence is evaluation.

Migration questions need the same precision. Do not just say "people move for jobs." Say who moves, from where to where, why, and with what result. Rural-urban migration by young adults can increase urban growth and pressure on housing, while rural areas may be left with labour shortages or a higher elderly share. International migration can slow ageing in receiving countries if migrants are mostly working age, but it can create political debate over housing, services, identity, and labour markets. In sending countries, remittances can support households, but selective out-migration of skilled workers may create brain drain. A balanced migration answer has both origin and destination effects.

Population distribution also links to physical and human factors. Dense populations often occur near coasts, rivers, fertile soils, transport routes, jobs, and services. Sparse populations may occur in deserts, mountains, cold regions, dense forests, or areas with poor access and limited employment. But do not make physical determinism the whole answer. Technology, government policy, history, colonial patterns, trade, conflict, and investment can reshape distribution. For example, a harsh physical environment may still support dense settlement if water, transport, and economic opportunity are managed effectively. A favourable physical environment may remain sparsely populated if jobs, infrastructure, or security are weak.

The best Paper 2 answers often connect Population to other core units. Population growth and urbanization can increase resource consumption, energy demand, food demand, and waste. Ageing can change health, housing, transport, and fiscal priorities. Climate vulnerability is affected by where people live, especially if dense populations are exposed to coasts, floodplains, heat stress, or drought. Migration may be partly shaped by climate stress, conflict over resources, or uneven economic opportunity. These links are useful in extended answers, but in short structured questions, keep them controlled and source-based.

Finally, watch out for words that are too broad. "Developed" and "developing" can be imprecise unless the source or question uses them. "Overpopulated" is also risky unless you define it as population pressure relative to resources and technology. "Poor country" is too vague; use indicators such as fertility, median age, mortality, migration, access to services, income, or infrastructure. "Better" and "worse" are weak unless you explain better for whom and by what measure. Replace broad labels with geographic evidence.

For quick revision, keep five sentence stems ready. "The data suggest a youthful structure because..." "This contrasts with an ageing structure shown by..." "This may create pressure on..." "However, the impact depends on..." "A possible policy response is..." Those stems cover describe, explain, and evaluate. They also stop you from jumping straight from data to unsupported opinion. In Paper 2, the fastest way to gain marks is not to write more; it is to make each sentence do a clear job.

Finish with common comparison points. Youthful versus ageing population. High fertility versus low fertility. Natural increase versus migration-led growth. Rural-urban migration versus international migration. Dense urban core versus sparsely populated rural or remote areas. High exposure to services demand versus high exposure to pension and healthcare demand. Demographic dividend versus dependency burden. Policy success versus policy limits.

For today's 25-minute block, Population is not the main written redo unless the May 3 Paper 2 population question was your weakest task. But keep it active. After the Urban remediation rewrite, do a two-minute population drill: write one data contrast, one DTM link, one consequence of a youthful structure, one consequence of ageing, and one reason replacement fertility does not immediately stop growth. If you can do those five things from memory, Population remains ready for Paper 2.
