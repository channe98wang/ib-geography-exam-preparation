4. b) Food insecurity is an incredibly significant problem, with around 2 billion people experiencing food insecurity globally. It's a multifaceted problem thats stems from multiple different sources. This makes it so that the strategies for improving food security and their effectiveness depends heavily on the source of food insecurity and an area's specific context.

One example of this is crop diversification through replacing monocultures with high value crops. This is a great way to combat malnutrition, through diversifying the types of nutrients that are getting consumed, as will as agricultural productivity, through improving soil quality and nutrient cycling in the soil by getting rid of monocultures. It also offers greater economic growth, which results in an increase in the ability to afford food, as well as food storage/transportation infrastructure as the country's economy grows. Such a case can be seen in Bangladesh, were the fertile soils of the country's lowlying deltaic geography allowed for the diversification of crops and an increase in high value crops. This effort, along with many other solutions, has made Bangladesh's agricultural sector the largest driver of poverty in Bangladesh, and has also increased the country's agricultural productivity significantly. However, this solution worked largely due to the country's physical climate and geography. An area where it wouldn't work is Niger, located in west Africa and housing the Saharan Desert in the north and the Sahel. Niger's economy is heavily based in agriculture, with 40% of the country's GDP coming from the sector and 87% of its employment stemming from the sector, It's climate is incredibly hot and arid, which does not make it optimal for agriculture. Thus, the country relies on growing millet and sorghum, which are significantly more drought resistant and requre less water than other plants. This means that Niger cannot sustain growing high value crops, such as fruits and vegetables, due to the high requirement of water and fertile soil needed.

Therefore, the sucess of a strategy, especially one rooted in agriculture, is highly dependent on the specific contexts of a country. A country's climate, population distribution, and economy, has great impacts on the sucess of a strategy. Thus, rather there being a strategy that works best for all countries suffering from poor health and food insecurity, there instead exists different strategies that cater to a country's specific context the best.
