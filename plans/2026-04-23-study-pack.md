## Audio 1 Script
### Course Map, Exam Structure, and Paper 1 Options

Target length: about `12 min`, close to the weekday audio target in the main
plan, with a natural revision pace and short pauses.

Today is not a deep memorization day. It is a map-making day. The point is to
stop seeing IB Geography as one large blur and start seeing it as five specific
study lanes. For this preparation window, those five lanes are `Urban
environments`, `Food and health`, `Population distribution - changing
population`, `Global climate - vulnerability and resilience`, and `Global
resource consumption and security`. If you can name the lanes, define what sits
inside each one, and tell the difference between them, later revision becomes
much faster. If you cannot, every study session feels random.

Start with the overall exam picture. In this repo's working plan, `Paper 1` is
the options paper and `Paper 2` is the core paper. Your selected options are
`Urban environments` and `Food and health`. That means the first written paper
asks you to work with human-geography themes where place, process, pattern,
inequality, and management are central. Then `Paper 2` moves to the core theme
of global change, where you need to connect population, climate, and resource
security. So there is a simple mental model for the whole course: `Paper 1`
asks, "How do specific human and environmental themes work in real places?"
`Paper 2` asks, "How do large-scale global processes reshape places and human
decisions?"

Make that structure concrete. `Paper 1` lasts `1h 30m` and counts for `35%` of
the final grade. Because you study two options, you answer both of them. For
each option, you do `1` compulsory structured question and then `1` extended
answer chosen from a `choice of 2` prompts. That means `4` answers overall in
`90` minutes. `Paper 2` lasts `1h 15m` and counts for `40%` of the final grade.
It requires `3` compulsory structured questions, one from each core unit, plus
`1` compulsory visual-stimulus task, plus `1` extended answer chosen from a
`choice of 2` prompts. The remaining `25%` of the course grade is the `Fieldwork
/ IA`, but this short revision plan assumes that component is already finished
and focuses on the written papers.

Why should you care about those numbers on Day 1? Because they explain the logic
of the whole schedule. `Paper 1` requires flexibility inside two option themes,
so the revision plan keeps pushing you to know subtopics, not just titles.
`Paper 2` is both heavily weighted and largely compulsory, so you cannot hide a
weak core unit and hope the exam lets you avoid it. If you remember nothing
else, remember these two summary lines: `Paper 1 = 4 answers in 90 minutes`.
`Paper 2 = 5 tasks in 75 minutes`. That is why today's goal is not only content
recognition. It is also strategic awareness of where you are most likely to lose
time or marks.

Another useful way to picture the subject is by question type. Geography rarely
rewards loose opinion. It rewards accurate vocabulary, clear causal chains, use
of evidence, and judgment. So whenever you revise a topic, ask four things.
First, can I define the key terms precisely? Second, can I explain the process?
Third, can I anchor the answer in a place or example? Fourth, can I make a
judgment instead of listing points? That four-part check will matter again and
again whether the topic is suburbanization, obesity, climate vulnerability, or
water stress.

Day 1 is also the right time to plant the command-term ladder you will keep
using across the next two weeks. `Define` means give a precise meaning.
`Describe` means state what is visible or what the pattern is. `Explain` means
show why something happens or how one factor leads to another. `Analyse` means
break the pattern or process into parts and show relationships. `Examine` means
look carefully at an issue from more than one angle. `Evaluate` means weigh up
strengths, limits, or competing interpretations before reaching a judgment. `To
what extent` means make the judgment explicit by saying how far a claim is true
and where its limits are. That ladder matters because geography answers often
lose marks not from missing facts, but from answering the wrong command.

Now focus on the first `Paper 1` option: `Urban environments`. At the broadest
level, this option is about how urban places are formed, how they change, what
pressures they create, and how they can be managed more sustainably. A city is
not just a big settlement. It is a spatial system with flows of people, money,
materials, information, transport, and power. Geography wants you to notice
patterns inside that system. Where do commercial functions cluster? Where is
high-value land? Where do migrants settle first? Where do transport bottlenecks
build? Which districts attract reinvestment, and which face neglect? Urban
geography is really the study of uneven change inside built environments.

The first cluster inside `Urban environments` is the variety of urban places and
their spatial patterns. That means you should be able to think about contrasts
between megacities, rapidly growing cities in low- and middle-income countries,
shrinking post-industrial cities, commuter suburbs, and informal settlements.
The same label, city, hides very different realities. Some are expanding
through manufacturing and service growth. Some are being reshaped by finance,
tourism, or logistics. Some are marked by sprawl and car dependence. Others are
densifying around transit. When a question mentions urban spatial pattern, it is
really asking you to see location, function, access, inequality, and land use
at the same time.

The second cluster is changing urban systems. This includes `urbanization`,
`migration`, `suburbanization`, `reurbanization`, `gentrification`, and
`deindustrialization`. You do not need to memorize them as isolated words. See
them as linked processes. Urbanization increases the proportion of people living
in urban areas. Migration moves people into, out of, or within cities.
Suburbanization pushes households and activities outward. Reurbanization can
pull investment and residents back into inner-city areas. Gentrification often
raises land values and changes the social mix of neighborhoods, sometimes with
displacement. Deindustrialization removes older employment bases and can leave
brownfield land, unemployment, and social stress behind. When you revise this
cluster later, keep asking: what is moving, why is it moving, who benefits, and
who loses?

It is worth noting one small syllabus-choice point here. The short plan summary
names `urbanization`, `migration`, `suburbanization`, `gentrification`, and
`deindustrialization`. This Day 1 pack also includes `reurbanization`
deliberately because current IB teaching materials commonly treat it as part of
the same cycle of urban change. So if you see it in class notes or revision
sources, do not treat it as off-topic. Treat it as part of the wider urban
systems story.

The third cluster is urban stress. Cities generate opportunity, but they also
concentrate risk. Environmental stress can include air pollution, waste,
pressure on water supplies, heat-island effects, traffic congestion, loss of
green space, and flood risk caused by impermeable surfaces. Social stress can
include housing shortages, informal settlements, inequality, segregation, crime,
uneven service access, and political tensions over land use. These stresses are
rarely random. They are usually tied to planning capacity, speed of growth,
income inequality, governance quality, and infrastructure gaps. A strong
geography answer moves beyond "cities have problems" and shows how specific
processes create specific stresses in specific places.

The fourth cluster is sustainable urban systems. This is where the course moves
from diagnosis to management. Sustainable urban strategies can include mass
transit, mixed land use, compact development, urban greening, waste reduction,
slum upgrading, flood-control design, cycling infrastructure, energy-efficient
buildings, and more inclusive planning. The key skill here is evaluation.
Almost no urban strategy is perfect. A metro system may cut congestion and
emissions but remain expensive or socially uneven. Urban greening may improve
liveability but also raise land values and contribute to gentrification. So the
best answers do not treat "sustainable" as a magic label. They ask, sustainable
for whom, at what scale, and for how long?

Now move to the second `Paper 1` option: `Food and health`. This option studies
how food systems and health outcomes vary across places, what causes those
patterns, which actors shape them, and how future food and health security might
be improved. The core geographical habit here is to see food and health not as
purely personal matters, but as spatial and political ones. Access is uneven.
Risk is uneven. Power is uneven. The systems that produce food or shape health
outcomes are linked across scales from the household to the global market.

The first cluster here is measuring food and health. That means being comfortable
with indicators and comparisons. Food can be measured through availability,
access, quality, nutrition, affordability, and stability over time. Health can
be measured through mortality, morbidity, life expectancy, infant mortality,
body-mass trends, disease incidence, and access to care. Be careful with single
numbers. A place can have enough calories on paper and still suffer malnutrition
because diet quality is poor or distribution is unequal. A place can have a high
average life expectancy and still contain severe internal health inequality.
Geography rewards students who treat indicators as evidence, not as complete
truth.

The second cluster is food systems and disease spread. A food system includes
inputs, production, processing, transport, retail, consumption, and waste. Once
you see that chain, you can explain why drought, conflict, fertilizer prices,
transport disruption, trade policy, or household income can all affect food
security. Health works in a similarly connected way. Disease spread is shaped by
population density, sanitation, mobility, nutrition, immunity, education,
housing, and healthcare access. Some questions will focus on infectious disease,
others on diet-related disease such as obesity, diabetes, or cardiovascular
problems. In both cases, the best answers do not isolate the illness from its
geographic context.

The third cluster is stakeholders. In `Food and health`, stakeholders matter
because decisions are made by people and institutions with different interests.
Governments care about public health, political stability, and budget limits.
Farmers care about yields, prices, land access, water, and risk. Agribusinesses
care about supply chains, markets, and profit. NGOs may focus on nutrition,
equity, education, or crisis response. Consumers make choices, but those choices
are constrained by income, culture, advertising, and availability. Whenever a
question asks about strategy or future change, stakeholder tension is usually
part of the answer. A policy can be efficient and still be politically difficult
to implement.

The fourth cluster is the future of food security, health, and sustainability.
This is not just about producing more food. It is also about reducing waste,
improving diets, protecting soils, managing water, improving supply-chain
resilience, reducing vulnerability to shocks, and dealing with the environmental
costs of production. A good evaluative answer might compare intensification,
technology, trade, local food systems, social protection, or dietary change.
Again, the key is not to hunt for one miracle fix. The key is to show that food
and health outcomes are shaped by interacting environmental, economic, and
political factors.

So what should you take away from this first audio? `Urban environments` asks
you to understand how cities are organized, how they change, what problems they
generate, and how they can be managed. `Food and health` asks you to understand
how food systems and health outcomes are measured, why they vary, who shapes
them, and what future responses are possible. The common thread is unevenness.
In both topics, geography is interested in why opportunities and risks are not
shared equally across space.

There is one more practical exam habit to attach to these two options. Do not
revise an option as if it were only a knowledge list. Revise it as if you may
need to use it in both a shorter structured response and a more evaluative
extended answer. That means every subtopic should eventually be stored in two
forms in your head. First, the fast version: key terms, process, and one clean
cause-and-effect chain. Second, the essay version: one named place or case,
competing stakeholders or interpretations, and a clear judgment. If you build
that dual format from the start, later mock papers feel much less abrupt.

Before ending, lock in a short exam habit. When you meet any new subtopic, do
not stop at the definition. Build a five-part frame. Term. Process. Pattern.
Example. Judgment. For example, if the term is `gentrification`, define it,
explain how reinvestment changes land values and social mix, identify the
pattern of neighborhood change, attach it to a place, and judge whether the
outcome is socially balanced. If the term is `food security`, define it, explain
how income and supply chains shape access, identify who is most exposed, attach
it to a place, and judge how effective the response is. That is the level of
thinking that turns revision into usable answers.

Pause now and ask yourself three questions from memory. First, what are the four
main clusters inside `Urban environments`? Second, what are the four main
clusters inside `Food and health`? Third, which of those clusters already feels
weak or vague? That last question matters most, because today's purpose is not
to prove mastery. It is to identify where mastery is missing.

## Audio 2 Script
### Paper 2 Core Overview

Target length: about `12 min`, close to the weekday audio target in the main
plan, with a natural revision pace and short pauses.

The second audio moves from options to the shared core theme: `Geographic
perspectives - global change`. This is the heart of `Paper 2`. The three units
you need active in memory are `Population distribution - changing population`,
`Global climate - vulnerability and resilience`, and `Global resource
consumption and security`. A good way to remember them is this: people, planet,
and provision. Population asks where people are, how population structures
change, and what that means. Climate asks how a changing physical system creates
uneven risk and unequal capacity to respond. Resources asks how consumption and
security are distributed, contested, and managed.

Start with `Population distribution - changing population`. Population geography
is not just counting people. It is about pattern, change, and consequence. Where
are people concentrated? Why are some areas densely populated while others are
sparsely populated? How do fertility, mortality, and migration alter population
size and structure? What happens when a society is youthful, ageing, shrinking,
or growing very rapidly? The real skill here is to connect data to meaning. A
population pyramid is never just a shape. It is evidence of social reality:
healthcare access, birth rates, dependency burden, labor supply, policy
pressure, and future demand for services.

Here is a quick way to make that skill concrete. Imagine a population pyramid
with a very wide base, quickly narrowing through the middle, and only a very
small elderly population at the top. That shape usually suggests high birth
rates, a youthful population, and likely rapid natural increase. In an exam
answer, do not stop at the visual description. Push it forward. A youthful age
structure can imply pressure on schools, healthcare, housing, and future job
creation. It can also imply a possible demographic dividend later if education
and employment expand successfully. Now imagine a very different pyramid: a
narrow base, wide middle, and relatively large older age groups. That points
toward ageing, lower fertility, and likely pressure on pensions, healthcare, and
labor supply. The exam skill is to read the shape, infer the process, and then
state the consequence. If you train that sequence early, population data becomes
much less intimidating.

When you revise this unit, keep five anchor ideas in mind. First, distribution:
how physical geography, climate, soils, water, coasts, transport, and history
shape where people live. Second, change: how natural increase and migration
alter population totals. Third, structure: age, sex, dependency, and workforce
composition. Fourth, policy: how states respond to growth, ageing, labor
shortage, or migration pressure. Fifth, inequality: population issues are never
experienced in exactly the same way by all groups or regions. If a place has an
ageing population, for example, one answer should move from the demographic fact
to pensions, labor supply, healthcare demand, rural service provision, and
immigration debate.

The same logic applies to distribution maps and migration graphs. If a map shows
population clustering near coasts, river valleys, or transport corridors, the
task is not just to say where people are. The task is to link settlement pattern
to opportunity, physical conditions, accessibility, and historical development.
If a migration graph shows large net in-migration into one city and out-migration
from another, the stronger answer links those flows to jobs, housing, conflict,
policy, or environmental stress. Population geography becomes easier when you
keep turning static evidence into dynamic explanation.

Now move to `Global climate - vulnerability and resilience`. This unit asks you
to think geographically about climate change rather than only scientifically.
Yes, causes and evidence matter, but the real emphasis is uneven impact and
response. Climate hazards do not produce the same outcome everywhere. Exposure,
wealth, infrastructure, governance, livelihoods, and social marginalization all
shape vulnerability. Two coastal places can face similar sea-level risk yet have
very different outcomes because one has stronger housing, insurance, early
warning systems, and evacuation capacity. So vulnerability is about sensitivity
and exposure, while resilience is about the capacity to absorb, adapt, recover,
and reorganize.

One terminology note helps here. Some geography sources treat `adaptive capacity`
as part of vulnerability itself, while others speak of vulnerability on one side
and resilience or coping capacity on the other. For IB SL, either convention can
work if you stay clear and consistent. The safe move in a timed answer is to be
explicit: say that risk is shaped by exposure, sensitivity, and the ability to
respond. That avoids a pointless terminology clash and keeps the focus on the
real geographical issue, which is uneven capacity between places and groups.

The essential structure of this unit can be remembered in four moves. Cause.
Impact. Vulnerability. Response. Cause includes the enhanced greenhouse effect
and the human activities driving it. Impact includes heat, drought, storms,
flooding, food stress, health stress, displacement, ecosystem loss, and economic
damage. Vulnerability explains why some people and places are hit harder.
Response includes mitigation and adaptation. Mitigation tries to reduce the
cause, such as through emission cuts or energy transition. Adaptation tries to
reduce the harm, such as through flood defenses, crop change, urban cooling, or
water management. Strong answers compare these strategies rather than treating
them as interchangeable.

Try a contrast example. Imagine two coastal locations exposed to storm surge and
sea-level rise. Place A is wealthy, has updated building codes, flood walls,
insurance coverage, effective forecasts, and evacuation routes. Place B has more
informal housing, weaker infrastructure, limited state support, and fewer
financial buffers. The physical hazard may be similar, but the human outcome can
be very different. Place B is likely to face greater housing loss, slower
recovery, and deeper long-term disruption. That is the core geographical lesson:
hazard is not the same as disaster. Disaster emerges when physical exposure meets
social vulnerability. A strong answer names both sides of that equation.

The word `resilience` deserves special care. It does not mean invulnerability.
It means capacity to cope and recover without collapsing into deeper crisis. A
community may still be damaged but recover faster because institutions are
strong, social networks function, and warning systems work. Geography likes this
concept because it forces you to think across scales. A household can be
vulnerable even in a wealthy country. A city can be partly resilient and partly
fragile. A national policy can look strong on paper and fail in a local context.
Whenever you use resilience in an answer, be concrete about what system is
resilient, resilient to what, and by what evidence.

The third unit is `Global resource consumption and security`. This is where the
core becomes strongly political. Resource geography asks who consumes, who
controls, who lacks access, and how security can be improved. Resources are not
only physical stocks. They are also shaped by technology, infrastructure, price,
trade, and power. One place may have water physically present yet still face
water insecurity because distribution systems are weak or contamination is high.
Another may import energy or food successfully because it is wealthy, even if
domestic supply is limited. Security therefore depends on both environment and
capacity.

When revising this unit, think in terms of demand, inequality, and management.
Demand grows with population, urbanization, rising incomes, and changing diets.
Inequality appears because high-consumption societies use far more energy,
materials, and water per person than low-consumption societies. Management asks
how states, firms, and communities respond through conservation, efficiency,
trade, infrastructure, technology, and governance. Many questions in this unit
work well if you remember the `water-energy-food nexus`: action in one resource
area often affects the others. More irrigation can improve food output but raise
energy use and pressure water security. Biofuels can change energy supply but
also compete with food production. Large dams can support water storage and
electricity while also displacing people or altering ecosystems.

Make that nexus idea concrete with a worked chain. Suppose a dry region tries to
boost food production through large-scale irrigation. Food output may rise, which
looks positive at first. But irrigation often requires pumps, canals, treatment,
or transfer systems that use energy. If groundwater is overused, water tables may
fall and long-term water security may weaken. If electricity for pumping comes
from fossil fuels, the strategy may also raise emissions. A policy that seems to
solve one problem can therefore intensify two others. Now reverse the example.
If a state invests in hydropower, it may strengthen energy supply and help reduce
fossil-fuel dependence, but the dam may flood farmland, alter river ecosystems,
or displace communities. The exam skill is to show the trade-off clearly instead
of talking about resources as separate boxes.

The phrase `resource security` should trigger a precise idea. It means reliable,
safe, and affordable access to essential resources over time. The word `over
time` matters. A place may seem secure in a normal year and become insecure
during drought, conflict, market shock, or infrastructure failure. This is why
geography often studies resilience in resource systems too. Security is not
simply owning the resource. It is maintaining access under stress.

This is also where scale matters. A country can appear resource secure at the
national level because it imports successfully, yet still contain households or
regions with severe insecurity because income, infrastructure, or service access
is uneven. The reverse can happen too: a place may have local water or food
availability but remain vulnerable because storage, governance, or affordability
is weak. Whenever you see the word `security`, ask, secure for whom, at what
scale, and under what conditions?

Now link the three core units together. Population change alters demand for
housing, food, water, transport, and energy. Climate change affects crop yields,
water availability, migration pressure, and disaster risk. Resource insecurity
can intensify social tension, shape migration, and make climate adaptation
harder. That is why `Paper 2` should never feel like three sealed boxes. The
highest-value revision comes when you can move between them. For example, if a
population grows rapidly in a dry region, that is immediately a population story,
a resource story, and often a climate-vulnerability story. Geography rewards
students who can see those overlaps.

There is also a shared exam skill across all three units: reading unfamiliar
material. Maps, graphs, tables, photographs, and infographics are not side
decorations. They are central evidence. So while revising, do not ask only, "Do
I know the content?" Also ask, "Can I pull a pattern from a graph in ten
seconds? Can I compare two places accurately? Can I use evidence instead of
vague claims?" If a graph shows high carbon emissions and high HDI in one group
of countries, the geographical task is not merely to describe that. It is to
interpret what it suggests about development pathways, inequality, and the
difficulty of transition.

One simple method helps with any stimulus. Start with `what`, then `where`, then
`why`, then `so what`. `What` pattern is visible? `Where` is it strongest or
weakest? `Why` might that pattern exist? `So what` does it imply for people,
policy, or inequality? That four-step sequence keeps you from getting stuck in
pure description. It also fits every core unit. A population graph, a climate
map, and a resource table all become more manageable when you force yourself to
move from observation to interpretation.

Here is a fast summary of the three units. `Population` is about distribution,
change, structure, and consequence. `Climate` is about causes, unequal impacts,
vulnerability, resilience, and response. `Resources` is about consumption,
security, inequality, and management. If you know those headline words, you
already have a frame for organizing later details.

Tie that summary back to the exam format. In `Paper 2`, the first three core
tasks are compulsory, which means the exam can test any one of these units
directly without giving you the option to avoid it. So your baseline revision
goal is modest but non-negotiable: for each unit, you want one clean process
explanation, one common visual pattern you can interpret, and one named example
or place angle you could mention under pressure. That is enough for Day 1. You
are not trying to master every detail tonight. You are trying to make sure none
of the three core units remains blank, passive, or psychologically avoided.

Finish with a diagnostic mindset. Ask yourself: in which unit can I already name
two subtopics confidently? In which unit can I already explain a process, not
just define a term? In which unit do I already have a usable place example or
case-study angle? And in which unit would I struggle to write even six accurate
sentences right now? Be honest. Day 1 is useful only if the rating is real.

One last reminder. Geography is a subject of connection. Population is not just
people. Climate is not just weather. Resources are not just commodities. Each
unit is about systems, unevenness, and decisions. If you carry that habit into
future revision, your notes become sharper and your answers become more
analytical.
