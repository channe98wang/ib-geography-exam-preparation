Audio 2 script: Climate infographic and policy evaluation practice

This audio prepares the 25-minute visual-stimulus task. The plan says Climate infographic and policy evaluation practice, so the focus is not a full climate content review. The focus is how to read a visual source quickly, turn it into marks, and evaluate a response without drifting into unsupported opinion.

Start with a rule: describe before explaining. In a visual-stimulus question, the source is the evidence. Do not ignore it. Read the title, units, categories, legend, scale, and command term. Ask: what is being measured, where are the highest and lowest values, what pattern is visible, and what exception or contrast stands out? Then write the answer in source language. A strong first sentence might be: North Delta has both high projected population growth, at 38 percent, and a high climate-risk index, at 86. That is better than "North Delta is vulnerable" because it gives the evidence first.

Use the "overall, high, low, exception" routine. Overall, risk may be highest where climate exposure combines with fast population growth, water stress, informal housing, or weak policy readiness. The highest value tells you the strongest pressure. The lowest value helps create contrast. The exception prevents a simplistic answer. For example, Wealthy Bay may have valuable coastal assets and a climate-risk score, but lower informal housing and stronger capacity can change the human impact. The same physical hazard does not produce the same outcome everywhere.

Climate risk is not only the hazard. It is hazard plus exposure plus vulnerability plus capacity to respond. Exposure means people, assets, infrastructure, or ecosystems are in harm's way. Sensitivity means how strongly they are affected: rain-fed farmers, elderly people during heatwaves, informal housing near floods, or water-stressed households may be more sensitive. Capacity to respond means warning systems, healthcare, savings, infrastructure, governance, education, insurance, evacuation routes, and social networks. When the question asks why similar climate-risk scores can create different human impacts, use this triangle: exposure, sensitivity, capacity.

Now add policy evaluation. A climate policy answer should not simply name a response. It should judge how well the response matches the risk. Flood defenses may reduce exposure to coastal flooding, but they are expensive, need maintenance, and may protect high-value districts more than informal settlements. Early warning systems can save lives, but they work only if warnings reach vulnerable groups and people can evacuate safely. Heat-action plans can target elderly people, outdoor workers, and low-income households, but they are limited if housing quality, electricity access, healthcare, and labour protections are weak. Drought-resistant crops can reduce food insecurity, but they need seed access, training, water management, credit, and market acceptance.

Separate mitigation and adaptation. Mitigation reduces greenhouse-gas emissions or climate drivers, such as renewable energy, efficiency, public transport, forest protection, or cleaner industry. Adaptation reduces harm from impacts that are happening or likely, such as flood shelters, water storage, crop change, heat planning, mangrove restoration, insurance, or relocation. A visual source about local risk usually needs adaptation and resilience. A broader policy question may also need mitigation. Evaluation should note that mitigation is essential long term, but it does not remove all present risk for already exposed populations.

Use "policy fit" as your evaluation phrase. Policy fit means the response matches the hazard, the exposed group, and the capacity gap. A water-stressed dry region needs water conservation, demand management, drought planning, crop adaptation, and possibly desalination or reuse depending on context. A low-lying delta needs flood warning, shelters, drainage, land-use planning, raised infrastructure, wetland or mangrove protection, and social support for displaced groups. A heat-prone city needs shade, cooling centres, healthcare outreach, labour protections, and energy reliability. A coastal import-dependent place may need port resilience, food and fuel security planning, emergency stocks, and diversified supply routes.

When reading an infographic, avoid three common mistakes. First, do not treat every index as a real percentage. If the source says "index," call it an index. Second, do not assume the place with the highest climate-risk score always has the worst human outcome. Human impact depends on housing, income, governance, health, and exposure. Third, do not overclaim beyond the source. If the source gives water stress and informal housing, use those indicators. Do not invent death tolls, costs, or migration numbers.

For the fast visual set, the question pattern is likely to be describe, suggest or explain, and state or evaluate. For describe, write two clear source patterns with numbers. For suggest, give plausible reasons linked to the source. For state, give one decision-maker and one decision. For evaluate, give benefit plus limitation. If you have 10 marks in 12 to 15 minutes, do not write an essay. Use short, precise paragraphs.

Here is a model describe answer. The highest climate-risk score is in North Delta, at 86, and it also has the highest projected population growth, at 38 percent, which suggests increasing exposure. Dry Coast has the highest water-stress index, at 91, even though its climate-risk index is lower than North Delta's at 74, so water scarcity is the clearest pressure there. Notice the answer gives two patterns, numbers, and interpretation without going too far into causes.

Here is a model suggest answer. Two regions with similar climate-risk indexes may have different human impacts because adaptive capacity differs. A region with stronger warning systems, healthcare, drainage, insurance, and emergency planning can reduce deaths and recover faster than a region with weak services. They may also differ because informal housing and livelihood dependence increase sensitivity; flooding or drought is more damaging where homes are insecure, incomes are low, or farming and water access are climate-sensitive. That answer uses vulnerability logic instead of simply saying one place is richer.

Here is a model policy evaluation answer. A decision-maker could prioritize flood shelters and early warning in a delta region with high population growth, high climate risk, and informal housing. This could reduce deaths and displacement during floods or storm surges. However, it is only partly effective if land-use planning, housing quality, drainage, and poverty are not addressed, because people may remain exposed between disasters. That answer gives policy, benefit, limitation, and a reason.

Now practise choosing policy from source evidence. If the highest value is food-system vulnerability, do not automatically choose a sea wall. Think about drought planning, crop adaptation, storage, food-price safety nets, irrigation efficiency, market access, and support for farmers or low-income consumers. If the highest value is health-system stress, think about heat-action plans, clinics, disease surveillance, cooling centres, clean water, sanitation, public warnings, and healthcare outreach. If policy readiness is low, do not write as if an expensive, complex megaproject can be implemented easily. Low readiness means the policy must be realistic: community warning, low-cost adaptation, external support, training, governance improvement, or phased investment may be more plausible than a high-tech solution.

Use comparison to evaluate. A region with climate-risk index 88 and policy readiness 42 may need urgent adaptation, but low readiness limits implementation. A region with climate-risk index 69 and policy readiness 64 may face lower physical risk but may be better able to implement a targeted heat plan. A strong answer does not just rank risk. It compares need with capacity. That is the heart of policy evaluation: where is the need highest, where is capacity weakest, and which response closes the gap most effectively?

Do not confuse policy readiness with absence of vulnerability. A region can have a higher readiness score and still contain vulnerable groups. A wealthy city may have hospitals, warning systems, and emergency budgets, but elderly people, outdoor workers, low-income tenants, or people without reliable electricity may remain highly exposed to heat. A port city may have stronger infrastructure, but import dependence can create resource and food-security risk if storms disrupt shipping. These details help you avoid simplistic "rich equals safe" answers.

For a 10-mark visual set, plan your time. Question 1 should be quick: two source patterns, each with data. Question 2 needs slightly more explanation: two reasons, each with a source link and a mechanism. Question 3 should be short but evaluative: one policy and one limitation. Do not spend eight minutes on Question 1 and leave Question 3 blank. A 2-mark question is easy marks if you name a policy and limitation clearly.

Here are sentence stems for the workbook. For Question 1: "The clearest pattern is..." and "A contrast is..." For Question 2: "One reason policy responses differ is..." and "Another reason is..." For Question 3: "A suitable response is..." and "A limitation is..." These are simple, but they prevent drift. The examiner should be able to see exactly which part of the question each sentence answers.

Now connect the visual skill to Food and health. Climate risk affects food through yields, pests, drought, flood damage, storage, transport, and prices. It affects health through heat stress, disease, malnutrition, water contamination, disaster injury, and mental stress. A visual source may show climate indicators, but you can still make a Food and health link if the question asks for human impacts. Use a chain: hazard -> exposed group -> food or health mechanism -> policy response -> limitation. For example: drought -> rain-fed farmers and low-income consumers -> lower yields and higher prices -> drought-resistant crops and safety nets -> limited by funding and access.

For policy evaluation, use four criteria. Effectiveness: does the policy reduce the risk shown? Equity: does it reach the most vulnerable groups? Feasibility: is it affordable, maintainable, and politically realistic? Sustainability: does it reduce long-term risk without creating new problems? A hard sea wall may be effective for one high-value coast, but less equitable if informal settlements remain unprotected. Desalination may increase water supply, but it can be costly and energy intensive. Relocation may reduce exposure, but it can disrupt livelihoods, community networks, and cultural ties. Mangrove restoration can reduce storm-surge energy and support ecosystems, but it needs space, time, protection, and local participation.

Now rehearse three policy-fit chains. Chain one: flooding in a delta -> high exposure of homes and infrastructure -> drainage, shelters, warning, and land-use planning -> fewer deaths and less displacement -> limited if informal housing and poverty keep people in risky places. Chain two: drought in a dryland region -> high food-system vulnerability -> water conservation, crop adaptation, storage, and safety nets -> more stable food access -> limited by low policy readiness, seed access, training, and funding. Chain three: heatwave city -> high health-system stress -> heat-action plans, shade, cooling centres, and healthcare outreach -> lower heat illness and mortality -> limited if housing is poor, electricity is unreliable, or outdoor workers cannot avoid heat. These chains are not meant to be memorized word for word. They are patterns you can adapt to any visual source.

Use one more distinction: response scale. Household responses include water storage, insurance, cooling behaviour, evacuation planning, or changing crops. Community responses include local warning networks, shelters, clinics, tree planting, food banks, or shared irrigation. National responses include regulation, infrastructure, finance, public health systems, land-use planning, and climate adaptation plans. International responses include climate finance, technology transfer, aid, and emissions agreements. Scale matters because a response may be effective at one level but insufficient at another. A household cannot build a national drainage system. A national policy can fail if households cannot afford to use it.

Finally, remember that a visual-stimulus answer must stay anchored to the source even when you use wider knowledge. Source first, geography second, policy third, evaluation fourth. If you reverse that order, the answer becomes generic. The fastest way to self-correct is to add a number or category from the source to the start of each answer. "Because Dryland Belt has food-system vulnerability of 91..." is stronger than "because drought is bad." "Because Heatwave City has health-system stress of 84..." is stronger than "because cities get hot." Use the numbers as anchors, then explain.

Use exact command terms. "Describe" means what the source shows. "Suggest" means a plausible reason from source plus knowledge. "Explain" means factor -> mechanism -> outcome. "Analyse" means relationships between hazard, vulnerability, response, and impact. "Evaluate" means judgement: how successful, for whom, where, and with what limitation. If the answer starts with the command term in mind, it will stay controlled.

End with a 30-second visual checklist. Title. Units. Highest. Lowest. Contrast. Exception. Command term. Policy fit. Benefit. Limitation. If you can run that checklist before writing, the visual-stimulus response becomes much less risky.

For the 25-minute block, use this rhythm. Spend three minutes reading the source and underlining indicators. Spend twelve minutes writing the 10-mark response set. Spend six minutes marking it. Spend four minutes writing one error-log sentence. The goal is not a perfect essay. The goal is speed, source evidence, and one real policy evaluation point.
